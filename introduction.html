Introduction to Laser Welding

Laser welding is a fusion welding process that uses concentrated light energy to melt and join materials. The laser beam creates a very small and intense heat zone, allowing high welding speed and precise control (The Welding Institute [TWI], n.d.).

Compared with many conventional arc welding methods, laser welding often produces lower distortion and narrow weld seams, especially in thin sections and precision parts (Agarwal, 2023). For this reason, it is commonly used in the automotive, electronics, and medical manufacturing sectors.

Why This Process Matters

  • Supports high-volume production with automation and robotics.
  • Allows strong, clean welds with minimal post-processing in many applications.
  • Can join difficult geometries in tightly controlled manufacturing lines.
  • Is increasingly important as modern products require lightweight precision parts.

Basic Principle

A laser source delivers energy through optics to a focused point on the workpiece. The metal melts, and the molten pool solidifies to form a weld joint. Shielding gas may be used to reduce oxidation and improve weld quality (American Welding Society [AWS], 2020).
